## 📋 Project Overview

This is Project 1 of the DecodeLabs UI/UX Design Internship (Batch 2026). The task was to act as a UX Researcher and build a complete empathy-driven research document for a fitness startup launching an app targeted at **busy office professionals** who consistently abandon existing fitness apps.

The core challenge: these users don't lack intention — they lack time, energy, and an app that understands their environment. This project defines who they are, what they feel, and how design should respond.

### Frame 02 — User Persona
**Purpose:** Humanise the target audience with a specific, research-grounded profile.

Introduces **Sarah Raza**, a 31-year-old Marketing Manager in Karachi. Rather than generic demographic data, the persona captures her goals, frustrations, behaviours, device context, tech literacy, and income — all of which directly inform design decisions.

**Key content:**
- **Name:** Sarah Raza · Age 31 · Karachi, PK
- **Occupation:** Marketing Manager · PKR 180K/month income
- **Goals:** Stay active without a gym · reduce stress · build a micro-habit · feel energetic by 6 PM
- **Frustrations:** Apps require 45–60 min · gamification feels patronising · no gym reachable · deletes apps in 2 weeks
- **Behaviours:** 4 coffees by noon · 7+ hrs seated · skips lunch · re-downloads deleted apps
- **Core Quote:** *"I want to feel healthy, but by 8 PM I'm too exhausted to care."*
- **Motivation type:** Mental Clarity (stress relief) — not Weight Loss (vanity)
- **Demographic tags:** Time-Poor · Corporate Professional · Mobile-First · Low Follow-Through

### Frame 03 — Empathy Map (4-Quadrant)
**Purpose:** Map Sarah's full user reality across the classic four dimensions.

Uses the standard empathy map framework — **Says / Thinks / Does / Feels** — with real, specific content rather than generic placeholders. The Says/Does quadrants capture external, observable behaviour. The Thinks/Feels quadrants capture the internal, hidden reality that traditional surveys miss.

**Key content:**
| Quadrant | Highlights |
|----------|-----------|
| **SAYS** | "I don't have time." · "I'll start Monday." · "It's too complicated." |
| **THINKS** | "I'm falling behind my peers." · "Why isn't there a 5-minute version?" · "What's wrong with me?" |
| **DOES** | Opens app at 7AM, closes at 7:02AM · skips lunch · scrolls health content at 11PM · re-downloads app after deleting it |
| **FEELS** | Guilt · Burnout · Shame · Hopelessness · Anxiety · Invisible (existing apps don't speak to her) |

---

### Frame 04 — Barriers to Entry
**Purpose:** Diagnose the three structural reasons Sarah ignores existing fitness apps.

Designed as a classified-document-style analysis slide. Each barrier is supported with specific, observable evidence and ends with a **Design Implication** — the direct UX response.

**Key content:**
| # | Barrier | Evidence |
|---|---------|---------|
| 01 | **Motivation Killers** | Back-to-back meetings · 40-min commute · pattern collapses by Friday |
| 02 | **Interface Friction** | Tutorial screens → immediate deletion · push notifications = guilt-tripping · minimal UI preference |
| 03 | **Physical Reality** | Open-plan office · nearest gym 4.5 km away · small apartment home · self-consciousness |

**Design Implications (bottom strip):**
- Sessions must be ≤7 minutes to fit existing calendar gaps
- Zero onboarding: open app → start workout in under 10 seconds
- All exercises must be desk-doable — no equipment, no floor needed

---

### Frame 05 — HMW & Insights
**Purpose:** Reframe pain points into design opportunities, then synthesise key research learnings.

The HMW (How Might We) method transforms user frustrations into actionable design briefs. Each HMW pairs directly with a documented pain point. The bottom section distils four key insights that should govern all subsequent design decisions.

**HMW Reframes:**
1. **Time** — HMW make 5-min micro-workouts feel as complete as a 45-min gym session?
2. **Interface** — HMW design an interface so frictionless that opening = starting the session?
3. **Physical access** — HMW create a desk-bound exercise library requiring zero equipment?
4. **Confidence** — HMW shift success metric from "did you work out?" to "did you move at all?"

**Key UX Insights:**
1. **Time is a Perception Problem** — Sarah has 5-minute gaps she doesn't call "workout time". The app must reframe, not create, time.
2. **Guilt is the Biggest UX Blocker** — Streaks and shaming push notifications accelerate churn. The tone must be consistently non-judgemental.
3. **Physical Context is Non-Negotiable** — Every exercise must work at an office desk in work clothes. The core constraint is the chair, not the gym.
4. **The Real Goal is Energy, Not Abs** — Value prop is "feel better by 3 PM", not weight loss. All copy and visuals must centre energy.

## 🧠 UX Principles Applied

### 1. Empathy-Driven Design
Every design decision in this document is grounded in the user's actual emotional and physical context — not assumptions. The 4-quadrant empathy map separates what users *say* from what they *think*, and what they *do* from what they *feel*, revealing the gap between stated intent and real behaviour.

### 2. Pain Point Identification
Rather than surveying surface-level preferences, this research identifies **structural barriers** — the system-level reasons users fail, not personal weaknesses. The three barriers (motivation, friction, physical access) are systemic and solvable by design.

### 3. HMW (How Might We) Reframing
Following IDEO's HMW methodology, each user frustration is reframed as a design question. This converts negative research findings into creative, actionable briefs that directly guide wireframing and feature prioritisation.

### 4. Context-First Design
The insight that all exercises must be desk-doable in work clothes is a *context constraint*, not a feature idea. This principle — designing for the user's worst-case environment, not their best-case — governs the entire product concept.

### 5. Emotional Tone Awareness
Recognising guilt as a UX blocker (not just a personal issue) informs microcopy strategy, notification design, and the choice to avoid streak-based mechanics — a direct application of emotional design principles.
